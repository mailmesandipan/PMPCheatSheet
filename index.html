📚 Knowledge Areas

Integration Management

The processes and activities needed to identify, define, combine, unify, and coordinate the various processes and project management activities within the Project Management Process Groups. It's about ensuring all parts of the project work together.

Read more

Scope Management

Ensuring the project includes all the work required, and only the work required, to complete the project successfully. It involves defining and controlling what is and is not in the project.

Read more

Schedule Management

The processes required to manage the timely completion of the project. This includes defining activities, sequencing them, estimating resources and durations, and developing and controlling the schedule.

Read more

Cost Management

Processes involved in planning, estimating, budgeting, financing, funding, managing, and controlling costs so that the project can be completed within the approved budget.

Read more

Quality Management

Processes for incorporating the organization's quality policy regarding planning, managing, and controlling project and product quality requirements in order to meet stakeholders’ expectations.

Read more

Resource Management

Processes to identify, acquire, and manage the resources (team members, equipment, materials, facilities, etc.) needed for the successful completion of the project.

Read more

Communications Management

Ensuring timely and appropriate planning, collection, creation, distribution, storage, retrieval, management, control, monitoring, and the ultimate disposition of project information.

Read more

Risk Management

Processes of conducting risk management planning, identification, analysis, response planning, response implementation, and monitoring risk on a project to increase the probability and/or impact of positive risks and decrease negative ones.

Read more

Procurement Management

Processes necessary to purchase or acquire products, services, or results needed from outside the project team. This includes contract management and change control processes.

Read more

Stakeholder Management

Identifying people, groups, or organizations that could impact or be impacted by the project, analyzing stakeholder expectations and their impact, and developing strategies for effectively engaging stakeholders in project decisions and execution.

Read more

🧮 Essential Formulas

Earned Value Management (EVM)
PV = Planned Value (Budget)
Example: Your project plan states that by the end of Week 4, work valued at $5,000 should be completed.
PV = $5,000
EV = Earned Value (Work Performed)
Example: At the end of Week 4, the actual work completed is valued at $4,500 based on the original budget.
EV = $4,500
AC = Actual Cost (Money Spent)
Example: The actual amount spent to complete the work by the end of Week 4 is $5,200.
AC = $5,200
BAC = Budget at Completion
Example: The total approved budget for the entire project is $50,000.
BAC = $50,000
SV = EV - PV (Schedule Variance)
Example: Using EV = $4,500 and PV = $5,000.
SV = $4,500 - $5,000 = -$500 (Behind schedule by $500 worth of work)
CV = EV - AC (Cost Variance)
Example: Using EV = $4,500 and AC = $5,200.
CV = $4,500 - $5,200 = -$700 (Over budget by $700)
SPI = EV / PV (Schedule Performance Index)
Example: Using EV = $4,500 and PV = $5,000.
SPI = $4,500 / $5,000 = 0.9 (Performing at 90% of the planned schedule rate)
CPI = EV / AC (Cost Performance Index)
Example: Using EV = $4,500 and AC = $5,200.
CPI = $4,500 / $5,200 ≈ 0.865 (Getting $0.865 worth of work for every $1 spent)
ETC = (BAC - EV) / CPI (Estimate to Complete - assuming current CPI continues)
Example: Using BAC = $50,000, EV = $4,500, CPI ≈ 0.865.
ETC = ($50,000 - $4,500) / 0.865 = $45,500 / 0.865 ≈ $52,589.60
EAC = AC + ETC (Estimate at Completion)
Example: Using AC = $5,200 and ETC ≈ $52,589.60.
EAC = $5,200 + $52,589.60 = $57,789.60
(Alternatively, if using EAC = BAC/CPI: $50,000 / 0.865 ≈ $57,803.47. Differences due to rounding.)
VAC = BAC - EAC (Variance at Completion)
Example: Using BAC = $50,000 and EAC = $57,789.60 (from AC+ETC).
VAC = $50,000 - $57,789.60 = -$7,789.60 (Projected to be over budget by this amount at completion)
TCPI = (BAC - EV) / (BAC - AC) (To-Complete Performance Index to meet BAC)
Example: Using BAC=$50k, EV=$4.5k, AC=$5.2k.
TCPI = ($50,000 - $4,500) / ($50,000 - $5,200) = $45,500 / $44,800 ≈ 1.0156
(To finish on budget, remaining work must be done at ~101.56% cost efficiency.)
Network Diagrams & Scheduling
Float/Slack = LS - ES or LF - EF
Example: An activity has Early Start (ES) = Day 5, Late Start (LS) = Day 7.
Float = LS - ES = 7 - 5 = 2 days
Or, if Early Finish (EF) = Day 9 and Late Finish (LF) = Day 11.
Float = LF - EF = 11 - 9 = 2 days
Early Start (ES) = Max(EF of predecessors)
Example: Activity C has two predecessors: Activity A (EF = Day 4) and Activity B (EF = Day 6).
ES for Activity C = Max(4, 6) = Day 6
Early Finish (EF) = ES + Duration - 1
Example: Activity D has an ES of Day 6 and a Duration of 3 days.
EF = 6 + 3 - 1 = Day 8 (Activity occurs on days 6, 7, 8)
Late Finish (LF) = Min(LS of successors)
Example: Activity E has two successors: Activity F (LS = Day 10) and Activity G (LS = Day 12).
LF for Activity E = Min(10, 12) = Day 10
Late Start (LS) = LF - Duration + 1
Example: Activity H has an LF of Day 10 and a Duration of 4 days.
LS = 10 - 4 + 1 = Day 7 (Activity must start by day 7 to finish by day 10)
Risk & Quality
Expected Monetary Value (EMV) = Probability × Impact
Example: A risk has a 20% (0.2) probability of occurring, and if it does, the cost impact is $10,000.
EMV = 0.2 × $10,000 = $2,000
PERT = (Optimistic + 4×Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 6
Example: Estimates for an activity: Optimistic (O) = 3 days, Most Likely (M) = 6 days, Pessimistic (P) = 15 days.
PERT = (3 + 4×6 + 15) / 6 = (3 + 24 + 15) / 6 = 42 / 6 = 7 days
Standard Deviation (SD) = (Pessimistic - Optimistic) / 6
Example: Using O = 3 days and P = 15 days from the PERT example.
SD = (15 - 3) / 6 = 12 / 6 = 2 days
Variance = Standard Deviation²
Example: Using SD = 2 days from the above example.
Variance = (2 days)² = 4 days²
Communications
Communication Channels = n(n-1)/2
Example: A project team has 6 members (n=6).
Communication Channels = 6 × (6-1) / 2 = 6 × 5 / 2 = 30 / 2 = 15 channels

🛠️ Tools & Techniques

Planning Tools
Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

A hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team to accomplish project objectives and create the required deliverables.

Read more
Network Diagrams (PDM/ADM)

Graphical representations of project activities and their logical relationships (dependencies). PDM (Precedence Diagramming Method) is most common.

Read more
Gantt Charts

A type of bar chart that illustrates a project schedule, showing the start and finish dates of activities and summary elements of a project.

Read more
Resource Histograms

A bar chart showing the amount of time that a resource is scheduled to work over a series of time periods, often compared against resource availability.

Read more
Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RACI)

A matrix that maps out who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed for tasks, milestones, or decisions on a project.

Read more
Risk Register

A document in which the results of risk analysis and risk response planning are recorded, including identified risks, their causes, potential responses, and owners.

Read more
Stakeholder Register

A project document including the identification, assessment, and classification of project stakeholders, along with relevant information about them.

Read more
Quality Tools (Seven Basic Tools)
Pareto Chart (80/20 rule)

A bar graph that ranks causes from most significant to least significant, helping to focus efforts on the "vital few" causes of problems.

Read more
Fishbone Diagram (Ishikawa / Cause-and-Effect)

A visualization tool for categorizing the potential causes of a problem to identify its root causes by exploring categories like People, Process, Equipment, etc.

Read more
Control Charts

Graphs used to study how a process changes over time, helping to determine if a process is stable and in control (within defined limits).

Read more
Flowcharts (Process Maps)

Diagrams that depict the sequence of steps and decisions in a process, used to understand, analyze, and improve processes.

Read more
Histogram

A bar graph showing the frequency distribution of numerical data, illustrating the shape and spread of the data.

Read more
Scatter Diagram (Correlation Chart)

A graph that plots pairs of numerical data to identify potential relationships or correlations between two variables.

Read more
Check Sheets (Tally Sheets)

A structured form for collecting and analyzing data in real-time at the location where data is generated, often used for frequency counts.

Read more
Risk Analysis Tools
Risk Probability/Impact Matrix

A grid for mapping the probability of risk occurrence against its impact on project objectives, used to prioritize risks for further attention.

Read more
Decision Tree Analysis

A diagramming technique for evaluating the implications of a chain of multiple options in the presence of uncertainty, helping to choose the best course of action.

Read more
Sensitivity Analysis (Tornado Diagram)

A technique to determine which risks have the most potential impact on a project's objectives, often visualized with a tornado diagram.

Read more
Monte Carlo Simulation

A quantitative risk analysis technique that uses random sampling and statistical modeling to simulate potential project outcomes and their probabilities.

Read more
SWOT Analysis

A strategic planning tool to identify Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats related to a project or business venture.

Read more
Root Cause Analysis (RCA)

A systematic process for identifying the fundamental causes of problems or incidents to prevent recurrence, rather than just addressing symptoms.

Read more
Data Analysis Techniques
Trend Analysis

Examining project performance over time to determine if performance is improving or deteriorating, using historical data to forecast future outcomes.

Read more
Variance Analysis

A technique for determining the cause and degree of difference between the baseline and actual performance, such as cost variance (CV) or schedule variance (SV).

Read more
Earned Value Analysis (EVA/EVM)

A comprehensive methodology that integrates scope, schedule, and resource measurements to assess project performance and progress against the baseline.

Read more
What-if Scenario Analysis

The process of evaluating different scenarios to predict their effect on project objectives, often used to assess the impact of changes or risks.

Read more
Reserve Analysis

Analyzing the project to establish reserves for budget and schedule (contingency and management reserves) to account for uncertainty and risks.

Read more
Performance Reviews

Meetings held to assess project status and progress by comparing actual performance to baselines, identifying issues, risks, and potential corrective actions.

Read more

💡 Exam Tips & Strategies

Before the Exam
• Review PMI Code of Ethics thoroughly
• Practice with realistic mock exams
• Focus on situational questions
• Understand the "PMI way" of thinking
• Get adequate sleep before exam day
During the Exam
• Read questions carefully, look for keywords
• Eliminate obviously wrong answers first
• Mark questions for review if unsure
• Use the brain dump sheet for formulas
• Take breaks every 90 minutes
• Trust your first instinct
Key Success Factors:
• Always think like a project manager, not a team member
• Follow proper processes (don't skip steps)
• Consider stakeholder communication in all scenarios
• Prevention is better than inspection
• When in doubt, choose the most professional/ethical option
• Servant leadership and collaboration are emphasized
Common Traps to Avoid
• Don't assume information not given in the question
• Avoid answers that skip process steps
• Don't choose answers based on your personal experience
• Beware of extreme words (always, never, all, none)
• Don't pick the first reasonable answer without reading all options
